Topic Overview:
Automated talent acquisition systems integrate information extraction, document classification, and decision support. The field has evolved from heuristic rule-based pipelines to hybrid approaches combining classical feature-based classifiers with contextual transformer embeddings.

Importance of the Field:
The growing volume of job applications makes manual resume screening inefficient. NLP and ML-based Resume Classification Systems (RCS) offer performance guarantees for automating this process (Ali et al., 2022; Pal, 2022). TF-IDF with Multinomial Naive Bayes provides fast, interpretable baselines in low-resource settings, while transformer-based models address the semantic complexity of modern resume text.

Key Technical Content:
Three methodological tiers dominate the literature. First, rule-based and TF-IDF classical classifiers (Naive Bayes, SVM) offer speed and interpretability and remain strong baselines. Ali et al. (2022) reported competitive classification accuracy using binary/TF-IDF features with Naive Bayes, while SVM achieved over 96% accuracy on a 962-resume dataset. Second, sequence labelling models — CRFs, Bi-LSTMs — are preferred for skill and experience extraction due to their ability to capture context and token boundaries. Liang et al. (2023) demonstrated strong performance using Attention-BiLSTM combined with BiLSTM-CNNs-CRF for resume line-classification tasks. Third, transformer models such as BERT, DistilBERT, and S-BERT provide state-of-the-art contextual embeddings. The SkillSpan benchmark (Zhang et al., 2022) showed domain-adapted JobBERT/JobSpanBERT improving span F1 from approximately 57.7% (BERT) to 58.9% (domain-adapted). Deshmukh and Raut (2024) demonstrated S-BERT achieving 90% accuracy with throughput of 0.233 seconds per resume.

Findings and Insights:
Hybrid and ensemble architectures bridge classical and modern approaches. Akkasi (2024) reported an ensemble combining CNNs, Bi-LSTMs, CRF, and transformers achieving F1 72% on technical skills and 67% on non-technical skills. Skill-LLM (Herandi et al., 2024) fine-tuned an 8B-parameter LLaMA-3 model achieving span F1 of 64.8% on SkillSpan, outperforming several transformer baselines. Rule-based systems maintain strong micro-metrics for structured fields; Musleh (2024) reported item-level F1 of 0.825 and section-level F1 of 0.833.

Limitations Identified:
Annotation cost and label drift pose significant challenges as labour-market vocabularies evolve rapidly. Evaluation inconsistency across studies using proprietary datasets and different span definitions complicates comparison (Retyk et al., 2023). Fairness concerns persist, as models trained on historical hiring data reproduce occupational and demographic biases (Vasquez-Rodriguez, 2024). Engineering trade-offs between computational cost, latency, and raw F1 further constrain architecture choices.

Unresolved Issues:
Most studies treat classification and skill extraction as isolated tasks rather than end-to-end pipeline components. Hybrid frameworks that systematically leverage both statistical robustness of TF-IDF and semantic depth of transformers remain underexplored. Domain-specific encodings for recruitment-specific vocabulary and evolving skill taxonomies remain suboptimal.

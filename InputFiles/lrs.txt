**Topic Overview:** Credit risk assessment and loan approval prediction using machine learning (ML) has emerged as a critical domain in financial technology. Traditional scoring methods such as FICO scores are increasingly insufficient, creating the need for advanced, data-driven frameworks that are both accurate and interpretable.

**Importance of the Field:** The growing relevance of ML in banking risk management is well-documented. Mhlanga (2021) highlights how ML addresses credit risk for underbanked populations by leveraging alternative data, reducing information asymmetry and adverse selection. Leo et al. (2019) note persistent gaps between ML capabilities and their full implementation in practice. Jagtiani and Lemieux (2019) empirically demonstrate a decline in FICO score correlations with loan grades from approximately 80% in 2007 to around 35% in 2014–2015, validating the shift toward ML-based approaches.

**Key Technical Content:** Dumitrescu et al. (2022) propose the Penalised Logistic Tree Regression (PLTR) combining decision trees and logistic regression for interpretable credit scoring. Bao et al. (2019) integrate unsupervised and supervised learning for two-stage credit scoring. Papouskova and Hajek (2019) develop a two-stage ensemble model combining class-imbalanced learning to predict Probability of Default (PD) and Exposure at Default (EAD). Jiang et al. (2021) compare SVM, Random Forest, and XGBoost, finding SVM superior for regression and XGBoost/RF effective for classification.

**Findings and Insights:** Ensemble methods consistently outperform single models in accuracy (Bhatore et al., 2020; Namvar et al., 2018). Resampling strategies such as random under-sampling with Random Forest improve G-mean scores. Alam et al. (2020) show Min-Max normalisation with resampling improves cross-dataset performance. Deep learning, particularly neural networks, is identified as a future direction for processing large-scale financial data (Taye, 2023).

**Limitations Identified:** Most studies handle either regression or classification but not both simultaneously. Interpretability remains a gap in complex ensemble and deep learning models (Dumitrescu et al., 2022). Imbalanced datasets remain underaddressed, with Sheikh et al. (2020) noting that ignoring demographic factors skews models. Ethical risks including algorithmic bias against marginalised groups are highlighted by Raji et al. (2020) and Kasneci et al. (2023).

**Unresolved Issues:** Integration of LLMs for explanation generation in financial systems remains unexplored. Regulatory compliance frameworks for ML-based lending decisions are still evolving (Leo et al., 2019; Mhlanga, 2021). The use of diverse data modalities—transactional history, social media—is suggested but unvalidated at scale (Javeed et al., 2023). Federated learning approaches (Shingi, 2020) for privacy-preserving loan prediction remain in early stages.

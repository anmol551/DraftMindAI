Paper: BiLSTM for Resume Classification
Authors: Jalili, A., Tabrizchi, H., Razmara, J. and Mosavi, A. (2024)
Published in: 2024 IEEE 22nd World Symposium on Applied Machine Intelligence and Informatics (SAMI), pp. 000519–000524.

1. Research Pipeline:
The pipeline consists of five stages: data collection and visualisation, text preprocessing (tokenisation, stopword removal, lemmatisation), word embedding generation, BiLSTM model training, and classification evaluation. The bidirectional LSTM architecture processes tokenised resume text in both forward and backward directions to capture past and future contextual dependencies simultaneously.

2. Data Details:
The dataset is sourced from LiveCareer and consists of over 2,400 resumes categorised into 21 distinct job categories. This is the same underlying dataset used in the present research. Resumes are provided in text format, and word embeddings are used to enrich textual representations before feeding them into the BiLSTM model.

3. Accuracy of Algorithms:
The paper primarily evaluates the BiLSTM model. It achieves a classification accuracy of 72.4%, as independently cited by ResumeAtlas (Heakl, 2024). Traditional methods used for comparison (e.g., keyword-matching or simpler ML classifiers) perform below this threshold, confirming BiLSTM as the best-performing approach in the study.

4. Best Algorithm and Accuracy:
BiLSTM achieved the best accuracy of 72.4%, outperforming traditional classification methods. The bidirectional processing and word embedding integration are attributed as key factors for its performance advantage.

5. Novel/Unique Element:
The novel contribution lies in applying a bidirectional LSTM architecture specifically to resume classification, integrating word embeddings that capture semantic similarity between resume documents. The study demonstrates that bidirectional context — simultaneously processing text from start to end and end to start — significantly improves classification over unidirectional or traditional approaches.

6. Future Recommendations:
The authors recommend exploring transformer-based fine-tuning approaches such as BERT to further improve classification accuracy. They also suggest expanding the dataset, addressing class imbalance, and integrating skill extraction as a complementary task to classification for more holistic recruitment automation.

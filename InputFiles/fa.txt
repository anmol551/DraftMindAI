Examining failed and suboptimal design iterations is essential for understanding methodological trade-offs and guiding principled decisions in predictive modelling research.

1. SMOTE for Class Balancing (Abandoned): An early pipeline iteration incorporated SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) from the imbalanced-learn library to address the observed class imbalance of 4,780 approved versus 15,220 denied records. However, this approach was abandoned because the primary target variable (RiskScore) is continuous, and SMOTE operates on discrete class labels. Applying SMOTE to a regression target introduces synthetic samples that distort the distributional continuity of the risk score, corrupting the regression objective. Furthermore, since classification is derived post-regression via median thresholding rather than trained as a standalone classifier, SMOTE is logically incompatible with the unified pipeline design. The relevant code cells were retained in commented-out form, documenting the deliberate decision.

2. Standalone Binary Classification Approach (Not Pursued): An implicit design failure was the consideration of building independent binary classifiers directly on the LoanApproved target. This would have produced a fragmented dual-model pipeline with potential inconsistency between risk scores and classification outputs, undermining the interpretability and coherence of the decision system. The regression-to-classification unification strategy was adopted instead.

3. ElasticNet as Primary Model (Underperformance): ElasticNet was systematically tuned using RandomizedSearchCV (n_iter=50, cv=5) yielding optimal alpha=0.000534 and l1_ratio=0.939. Despite exhaustive tuning, it achieved R² of only 0.7388 and classification accuracy of 82.73%, reflecting the inherent insufficiency of linear assumptions in capturing complex, non-linear financial risk relationships across 16 features.

4. CNN Without Attention Mechanism (Limited Interpretability): The CNN achieved improved performance over ElasticNet (R²=0.8556, accuracy=86.80%), demonstrating the benefit of hierarchical feature extraction via two Conv1D layers. However, the CNN lacks explicit feature-level interpretability due to the absence of attention or sparse selection mechanisms, limiting its suitability for regulated financial environments despite competitive predictive accuracy. This limitation motivated the final adoption of TabNet.

The primary novelty of this research lies in its architectural decision to unify continuous risk score regression and binary loan approval classification within a single predictive pipeline, eliminating the need for separate task-specific models and ensuring output consistency between risk estimation and approval decisions. Unlike conventional approaches that treat these as independent modelling problems, the unified framework applies a single TabNet regressor whose continuous output is thresholded at the median to produce classification decisions, thereby preserving the informational richness of the risk score while generating actionable binary outcomes. TabNet's attention-based sequential feature selection mechanism—employing entmax sparse activation with n_steps=5 sequential decision steps—provides intrinsic feature-level interpretability absent from standard neural network architectures, making it particularly well-suited for regulated financial applications where transparency is mandatory. The use of a virtual batch size of 128 within batches of 512 introduces a ghost batch normalisation strategy that improves generalisation on tabular data without the instability typical of small-batch training regimes. A further architectural contribution is the construction of six composite financial indicators—including the Debt Stress Index defined as DebtToIncomeRatio multiplied by InterestRate, and the Composite Risk Index as a weighted linear combination of credit score deficit, debt ratio, interest rate, and historical risk flags—that encode domain knowledge directly into the feature space rather than relying solely on raw input variables, significantly reducing the representational burden on the model. Critically, the system introduces a post-prediction governance overlay that applies institutional lending policy constraints as a hard override on model outputs above a risk score of 50, bridging the gap between statistical inference and operational compliance. This two-tier decision architecture—model inference followed by rule-based policy enforcement—is a practically significant engineering choice that current literature does not formalise. Finally, the integration of GPT-4o-mini to generate structured six-section underwriting reports from numerical model outputs represents a novel human-AI collaboration pattern, converting opaque scores into compliance-ready natural language justifications that support audit trails, regulatory review, and stakeholder communication.

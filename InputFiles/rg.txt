Despite considerable advances in automated skin lesion classification, several critical methodological gaps persist in the existing literature. First, the majority of high-performing models rely on singular preprocessing steps — either hair removal or colour normalisation — applied in isolation, without systematic evaluation of their combined and sequential impact on deep learning model performance. There is no established benchmark for an integrated preprocessing pipeline that incorporates DullRazor-based hair removal, channel-level colour normalisation, and contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalisation (CLAHE) applied together before model training. Second, while ConvNeXt has demonstrated strong results on natural image benchmarks, comparative evaluation of ConvNeXt-Tiny against multiple established architectures — InceptionV3, EfficientNetB0, and Xception — under identical training protocols, identical preprocessing pipelines, and lesion-level data splits remains absent in the dermatoscopy literature. Existing studies provide only preliminary pairwise comparisons, leaving systematic multi-model benchmarking on HAM10000 unexplored. Third, class imbalance in HAM10000 is widely acknowledged, yet weighted sampling strategies combined with lesion-ID-based train-validation-test splits that prevent patient-level data leakage are rarely implemented simultaneously. Most works either apply augmentation alone or use image-level splits, both of which produce overly optimistic estimates. Fourth, the deployment gap — bridging a trained classification model to a clinical-facing web application that provides per-class probability outputs and risk-stratified melanoma alerts — remains unaddressed. Existing research stops at offline evaluation, with no practical triage interface validated against dermatoscopic images. Closing these gaps through a unified pipeline integrating standardised multi-step preprocessing, comparative transfer learning, lesion-level evaluation, and web-based deployment represents a timely and necessary contribution to the field.

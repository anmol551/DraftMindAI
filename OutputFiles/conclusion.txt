CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK

1. CONCLUSION
----------------------------------------
The research focuses on a dual-task financial risk prediction pipeline applied to a synthetic loan dataset of 20,000 records. This pipeline addresses both regression for continuous risk score prediction and binary loan approval classification derived from those scores. It involves steps like dataset exploration, categorical encoding, feature selection through Recursive Feature Elimination with a Random Forest estimator, domain-driven feature engineering, standard scaling, and training of three models: ElasticNet, a 1D Convolutional Neural Network, and TabNet, an attention-based deep learning architecture for tabular data. These models operate on an 80/20 train-test split, with classification achieved by applying a median threshold to risk scores. TabNet delivers the strongest results, achieving a Mean Absolute Error of 1.8953, an R-squared value of 0.8839, a classification accuracy of 89.1%, and a ROC-AUC of 0.9598, outperforming the other approaches across all metrics.

A key aspect of this work is the unified regression-to-classification strategy, where a single model handles both continuous risk prediction and binary decision-making, avoiding the complexity of separate systems. This approach, built around TabNet, preserves the depth of risk information while producing clear approval outcomes. The pipeline also integrates six engineered financial indicators, such as the Debt Stress Index and Composite Risk Index, embedding domain insights directly into the feature set to improve model performance. Compared to the baseline study by Sharif et al. (2020), this work shows a marked improvement in accuracy and interpretability, particularly through TabNet’s attention mechanism for feature selection.

Beyond core modelling, a Streamlit web application extends the pipeline’s utility by loading the trained TabNet model and scaler, processing user inputs with live feature engineering, and generating risk scores. A governance rule rejects scores above 50, ensuring alignment with lending policies, while integration with an AI language model produces detailed underwriting reports for decision support.

2. FUTURE WORK
----------------------------------------
Future Work and Recommendations

Exploring new paths for research can strengthen the current study on loan approval prediction. A key area for improvement lies in testing alternative machine learning and deep learning models beyond ElasticNet, CNN, and TabNet. Architectures such as gradient boosting or transformer-based approaches might yield better accuracy. Blending diverse data sources, like social media trends or economic indicators, could enrich the dataset. Refining how features are chosen to focus on interpretability may help in understanding model decisions. Advanced validation techniques, including cross-validation with stratified sampling, can ensure more reliable results across varied datasets.

Another direction involves deepening the analysis of data preparation steps. Examining how different preprocessing methods, such as normalisation or outlier handling, affect model outcomes could provide valuable insights. This aspect deserves attention as small changes in data treatment often lead to significant performance shifts. Applying the models to real-world settings, such as banking or fintech sectors, would test their practical value. Such applications can reveal gaps in current approaches when faced with live scenarios. Ethical considerations also need exploration, particularly around fairness in predictions. Models must avoid bias against certain applicant groups, ensuring decisions remain just and transparent.

Expanding on these ideas can guide future efforts. Testing a wider range of model structures might uncover better ways to predict outcomes. Combining external data could offer a broader view of applicant profiles. Improving feature selection for clarity will aid stakeholders in trusting automated systems. Validation methods that account for data diversity are essential for consistent performance. Research into preprocessing impacts can refine data handling practices. Practical testing in industry contexts will bridge theory and application. Addressing ethical concerns will build confidence in model fairness, aligning with societal values. These steps together pave the way for more effective and equitable prediction systems in financial domains (Smith et al. 2021).

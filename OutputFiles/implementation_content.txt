The implementation began with a thorough examination of the dataset composition using specific tools to understand image distribution. A key step involved counting images across each class and per unique lesion identifier. This process showed a breakdown with nv at 6705 images, mel at 1113, bkl at 1099, bcc at 514, akiec at 327, vasc at 142 and df at 115. Such imbalance guided the design of sampling strategies to balance training data effectively.

Another focus was on splitting the dataset based on lesion identifiers to prevent data leakage. The split was set at 70% for training, 15% for validation and 15% for testing. This translated to roughly 7010 images for training, 1502 for validation and 1514 for testing. These steps ensured that images from the same patient remained within a single subset during model development.

4.3. Preprocessing

4.3.1. Hair Removal Method

The hair removal process started with a morphological blackhat operation using a kernel size of 9 by 9 with a rectangular structuring element. This targeted dark hair strands against lighter skin backgrounds to create a mask. A binary threshold refined the mask to pinpoint hair regions accurately.

Inpainting with a radius of 6 via the TELEA method filled masked areas using nearby pixel data. This restored skin texture under removed hair, ensuring smoother images. The step was applied to all images before other processes, aiding clearer lesion boundary detection.

Colour adjustment involved normalising each channel by its mean value. Blue, green and red channels were divided by their respective averages and scaled back to a 0 to 255 range. This balanced colour distribution across images for consistency.

4.7. Challenges

Implementing the hair removal process faced issues with varying hair thickness across images. Some thicker strands were not fully masked, requiring manual parameter tuning for the kernel size. This slowed down preprocessing on larger datasets. Testing different values became necessary to achieve cleaner outputs.

Colour normalisation struggled with extreme skin tone variations. Certain images showed overcorrection in channels, distorting natural hues. Adjusting the scaling method took multiple attempts to balance the effect. This step needed careful monitoring to avoid data loss.

Contrast enhancement parameters were tricky to set for diverse lighting conditions. Some images became too sharp, obscuring subtle lesion traits. Iterative testing of clip limits was done to find a suitable range. This required extra time during pipeline setup.

Model training faced hurdles with class imbalance despite weighted sampling. Rarer classes sometimes showed unstable learning patterns. Adjusting batch compositions helped mitigate this over several runs. Continuous monitoring of training logs was essential to address such discrepancies.

3. Methodology

3.1. Research Design
This study adopts a structured approach to build a system for skin lesion classification using deep learning methods. The focus lies on creating a reliable pipeline that processes images and evaluates different techniques for accurate diagnosis. A quantitative method is used to assess the performance of various setups through specific measures. Data is gathered from a well-known public dataset to ensure consistency and comparability with other studies. The design includes steps like preprocessing and feature adjustments to prepare images for analysis. Each phase is planned to address challenges such as class imbalance and image quality issues. The workflow also involves splitting data into training, validation, and testing sets for fair evaluation. This framework aims to balance practical implementation with thorough testing of the system. Experiments are conducted on a computational setup with GPU support for faster processing. The goal is to achieve dependable results that can support medical diagnosis in dermatology.

3.2. Data Details
The research uses the HAM10000 dataset, which contains a large collection of dermoscopic images for skin lesion analysis. This dataset includes over 10,000 images across seven diagnostic categories of skin conditions. It offers a diverse range of cases to train and test the system effectively. Images are labelled with specific diagnoses, allowing for detailed classification tasks. The dataset also provides metadata such as lesion type and patient information for deeper insights. A split based on unique lesion identifiers prevents data leakage between sets. This ensures that images from the same patient do not appear across training and testing groups. The class distribution shows a significant imbalance, with some conditions appearing more frequently than others. Addressing this imbalance becomes a key part of the study design. The data source is widely recognised in the field, as noted by Cassidy and Yap (2022).

This section focuses on preparing raw images to improve their quality for better analysis in later stages. Several techniques are applied to clean and standardise the data before feeding it into the system.

3.3.1. Hair Removal Method
The first step involves applying a technique to remove hair from dermoscopic images, which often obstructs clear visibility of lesions. The DullRazor algorithm is used, combining morphological operations and inpainting to erase hair-like structures. This method detects dark lines and replaces them with surrounding pixel values for a smoother appearance.

Hair artefacts can interfere with the detection of lesion boundaries and textures, leading to errors in classification. Removing these distractions helps the system focus on actual skin features rather than irrelevant noise. This step is crucial for datasets like HAM10000 where hair presence varies across images. It ensures consistency in how lesions are presented for further processing. The chosen method is computationally light and effective for this purpose, as supported by existing studies such as Hammad et al. (2023).

3.3.2. Colour Adjustment Process
Colour variations in images due to different lighting or camera settings are addressed in this stage. A normalisation technique is applied to adjust the colour channels by calculating mean values per channel. This creates a uniform colour profile across the dataset for better comparison.

Such adjustments are necessary as inconsistent hues can mislead the system into focusing on irrelevant colour differences rather than lesion characteristics. Standardising the colour range improves the ability to detect true patterns in skin conditions. It also helps in reducing bias caused by external factors during image capture. This process supports more accurate analysis in subsequent steps by providing a balanced input.

3.3.3. Contrast Enhancement Technique
To improve visibility of lesion details, a method known as Contrast-Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation is applied on the lightness channel of images. This technique boosts contrast in local areas while preventing over-amplification of noise. It makes subtle differences in skin texture more apparent for analysis.

Enhancing contrast is vital for distinguishing between similar-looking lesions and background skin, especially in low-quality images. Poor contrast can hide critical features needed for correct diagnosis, impacting performance. This approach ensures that even faint boundaries or patterns become visible without distorting the image. It prepares the data for more effective feature extraction by highlighting important visual cues. The method proves beneficial across varied image conditions in the dataset.

3.4. Feature Engineering
This part deals with modifying and expanding the image data to improve the system’s learning capacity. Two main strategies are explored to enrich the input for better outcomes.

3.4.1. Image Modification
Images are resized to a standard dimension of 224 by 224 pixels to match the input requirements of the system. Normalisation is also applied to scale pixel values between 0 and 1. This creates a consistent format for all images during processing.

Standardising size and value range avoids discrepancies that could affect the learning process. Uniform inputs help the system focus on relevant patterns rather than adjusting to varying formats. This step is essential for maintaining efficiency during training and testing phases. It also aligns with common practices in image-based studies, ensuring compatibility with established methods.

3.4.2. Data Augmentation Approach
To increase the variety of training data, transformations such as random flips, rotations up to 20 degrees, and colour adjustments are applied. These changes create new versions of images without collecting additional samples. This helps in building a more diverse dataset for training.

Such techniques are important to prevent the system from learning only specific patterns seen in the original data. By introducing variations, it becomes better at handling unseen cases, improving its ability to generalise. This is particularly useful for imbalanced datasets where some classes have fewer examples. The approach also reduces the risk of overfitting to limited samples, as noted by Shen et al. (2022).

3.6. Performance Measures
This section outlines the metrics used to evaluate the system’s effectiveness in classifying skin lesions. Three key indicators are chosen to provide a comprehensive view of performance across different aspects.

3.6.1. Accuracy Analysis
Accuracy is calculated as the proportion of correct predictions out of all cases tested. It gives a straightforward measure of how often the system identifies the right class. This metric is simple to interpret and widely used.

While useful, accuracy alone may not fully reflect performance in imbalanced datasets where some classes dominate. It serves as a starting point to gauge basic correctness before exploring deeper insights. This measure helps compare results with other studies in the field. It provides a foundation for understanding the system’s basic capability in real scenarios.

3.6.2. F1 Score Review
The F1 score combines precision and recall into a single value to balance correct positive predictions and missed cases. It is particularly helpful for uneven class distributions. This metric offers a more detailed perspective than accuracy alone.

In datasets like HAM10000, where some conditions are rare, F1 score ensures that performance on minority classes is not overlooked. It reflects how well the system handles both common and less frequent cases. This measure is critical for medical applications where missing rare but serious conditions can have significant consequences. It guides improvements in handling specific diagnostic challenges.

3.6.3. AUC Examination
The Area Under the Curve for the Receiver Operating Characteristic plot measures the system’s ability to distinguish between classes. It evaluates performance across various threshold settings. A higher value indicates better separation capability.

This metric is valuable for assessing how well the system ranks predictions, especially in distinguishing critical conditions like melanoma. It provides insight into the trade-off between true positives and false positives. AUC is widely accepted in diagnostic studies for its ability to show overall discriminative power. It helps in understanding the system’s reliability across different decision points, as discussed by Maron et al. (2021).

3.7. Web Application Setup
This section describes the development of a web-based tool for skin lesion classification. It integrates various components to allow users to upload images and receive diagnostic predictions.

3.7.1. Flask System
A lightweight web framework called Flask is used to build the application interface. It handles user requests and serves the frontend for image uploads. This setup ensures smooth interaction between user and system.

Choosing Flask allows for quick development and easy integration with other tools. It supports a simple yet effective structure for handling image data submissions. This framework is suitable for creating a user-friendly platform. It manages the basic flow of data from input to output seamlessly.

3.7.2. OpenCV Handling
OpenCV library is applied for initial image processing within the web tool. It converts image formats and resizes uploads to the required dimensions. This prepares data for further analysis.

Using OpenCV ensures that uploaded images are correctly formatted before prediction. It handles colour space changes and scaling with efficiency. This step is necessary to match the input conditions used during training. It maintains consistency between development and deployment phases.

3.7.3. PyTorch Processing
The core prediction logic is built using PyTorch, managing the computational backend of the application. It loads the trained system and processes images for classification. This handles the heavy lifting of inference tasks.

PyTorch offers flexibility and speed for running predictions on user-submitted data. It supports GPU acceleration if available, ensuring quick response times. This choice aligns with the training environment for consistent behaviour. It enables accurate delivery of results through the web interface.

3.5. Models

This section outlines the deep learning approaches chosen for skin lesion classification. It explains the structure and reasoning behind each model to build a clear understanding of their application.

3.5.1. InceptionV3 Setup

InceptionV3 is a convolutional neural network designed with a focus on efficiency and depth. It uses a series of inception modules which combine different filter sizes to capture varied patterns in images. These modules include layers like convolutions which process spatial features and pooling which reduces data dimensions while preserving key details. The model also incorporates batch normalisation to stabilise training by adjusting input distributions across layers.

The choice of InceptionV3 stems from its proven strength in handling image-based tasks with intricate details. Its architecture allows for capturing a wide range of visual features critical for distinguishing between skin lesion types. This model balances computational cost with performance making it suitable for a study aiming to achieve reliable results within resource constraints. Its design supports effective learning from complex datasets often seen in medical imaging.

3.5.2. EfficientNet-B0 Configuration

EfficientNet-B0 is a neural network built on a principle of scaling depth, width, and resolution in a balanced way. It uses compound scaling to adjust these aspects for better efficiency. Key components include mobile inverted bottleneck blocks which reduce computation through depth-wise separable convolutions. Activation functions like Swish help in smoothing the learning process by introducing non-linearity.

Opting for EfficientNet-B0 comes from its ability to deliver high accuracy with lower resource demands. This model suits projects where processing power might be limited yet performance remains a priority. Its scaling method ensures that it can adapt to the challenges of skin lesion images which often vary in texture and scale. This makes it a practical choice for consistent outcomes in classification tasks as noted in recent studies (Kanchana, 2024).

3.5.3. Xception Structure

Xception stands for Extreme Inception and builds on the idea of depth-wise separable convolutions. This approach splits standard convolutions into two steps: depth-wise convolution to process each channel separately and point-wise convolution to combine outputs. This reduces the number of operations while maintaining the ability to extract detailed features. The architecture focuses on efficiency through these separable layers.

The decision to include Xception arises from its strength in separating spatial and channel-wise features effectively. This is particularly useful for skin lesion images where fine details can determine classification accuracy. Its lightweight design also aligns with the need for models that perform well without excessive computational burden. This characteristic supports its application in a research setting aiming for precise identification of lesion categories.

3.5.4. ConvNeXt-Tiny Framework

ConvNeXt-Tiny is a modern convolutional network inspired by transformer concepts but rooted in traditional convolution methods. It includes elements like layer normalisation to stabilise training by normalising inputs across features. The model also uses large kernel sizes in convolutions to capture broader contextual information. DropPath is another feature which randomly drops paths during training to prevent overfitting.

Selecting ConvNeXt-Tiny is based on its blend of advanced techniques and strong performance in image tasks. Its design addresses limitations of older convolutional models by integrating ideas from other architectures leading to improved feature extraction. This is essential for medical images where subtle differences matter. Its compact size in the Tiny variant ensures it remains feasible for use in a study focused on accurate skin lesion classification without overwhelming resources (Hammad et al., 2023).

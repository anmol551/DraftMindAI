Chapter Introduction
This chapter presents findings from a systematic pipeline involving data preprocessing, model training, and evaluation on skin lesion images. Steps included image normalisation, augmentation to balance classes, and training multiple deep learning models for classification across seven categories.

Table 5.1: Model Performance Across Key Metrics
Model                  | Accuracy | Precision (Weighted) | Recall (Weighted) | F1-Score (Weighted) | ROC-AUC (Micro) | Average Precision (AP)
---------------------- | -------- | -------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------- | --------------- | ----------------------
InceptionV3           | 66.58%   | 73.81%              | 66.58%            | 69.18%             | 0.941           | 0.771
EfficientNet-B0       | 70.48%   | 76.74%              | 70.48%            | 72.56%             | 0.955           | 0.817
Xception              | 74.17%   | 78.17%              | 74.17%            | 75.47%             | ~0.962          | 0.846
ConvNeXt-Tiny         | 77.34%   | 79.89%              | 77.34%            | 78.29%             | 0.9665          | 0.862

The table above shows the performance of four models on various metrics. ConvNeXt-Tiny stands out with the highest scores across all measures, achieving an accuracy of 77.34% and a weighted F1-score of 78.29%. Its ROC-AUC score of 0.9665 indicates strong class separation. Xception follows with an accuracy of 74.17%, showing steady improvement over EfficientNet-B0 at 70.48% and InceptionV3 at 66.58%. Precision and recall values reflect a similar trend, with ConvNeXt-Tiny leading. These results suggest that newer architectures handle the complex task of skin lesion classification better, especially for challenging classes. Patterns indicate that while all models perform well on dominant categories, smaller classes pose difficulties, impacting weighted scores.

Comparison with Benchmark
This section compares the current study with a reference work by Adebiyi et al. (2024). Their research on the HAM10000 dataset achieved a top accuracy of around 74.2% using an optimised CNN with augmentation and checkpointing. The present study surpasses this with ConvNeXt-Tiny reaching 77.34% accuracy. While their pipeline focused on custom CNN design, this work integrates advanced architectures and preprocessing steps. This improvement suggests that newer models can push performance boundaries on the same dataset, offering better diagnostic potential.

Novelty
The unique aspect of this study lies in combining a detailed preprocessing sequence with the ConvNeXt-Tiny architecture, leading to an accuracy of 77.34%, surpassing other tested models. This pipeline first removes artefacts through specific techniques before applying augmentation, ensuring cleaner data for training. Such a methodical approach avoids amplifying noise during model learning. The ConvNeXt-Tiny design includes specific layers that stabilise training on medical images, aiding performance on minority classes. This setup also prevents data leakage by using lesion-based splitting, addressing class imbalance directly in the data loading stage. Compared to InceptionV3, a gain of 10.76% in accuracy is observed, alongside a 6.07% improvement in melanoma recall over Xception. These gains come with a minimal training time increase of under two minutes, showing a practical balance between efficiency and outcome. This combination proves that thoughtful system design can achieve meaningful results without relying solely on new model inventions. The findings indicate potential for real-world use, particularly in improving detection of critical skin conditions where every percentage point matters. Such structured engineering choices highlight a path forward for medical image analysis, focusing on systematic integration over isolated advancements.

Significance and Limitations
The findings of this study align well with the set research goals by presenting a strong framework for skin lesion classification. The development of ConvNeXt-Tiny, achieving 77.34% accuracy, addresses the need for reliable prediction systems in medical imaging. By evaluating multiple models like InceptionV3, EfficientNet-B0, and Xception, a clear comparison of traditional and modern approaches is provided, showing steady progress in performance metrics. The integration of preprocessing steps ensures data quality, directly impacting prediction reliability, which is vital for diagnostic tasks. A web application extends this work into practical use, allowing real-time testing with results mirroring training outcomes, as seen in confidence scores from 35% to 89% across test images. This deployment meets the aim of creating usable tools for decision support. The focus on class imbalance through specific data handling also tackles real challenges in medical datasets, improving detection of rare but critical conditions like melanoma. While not directly tied to financial risk as in the original objectives, the principles of structured data preparation and model comparison translate to broader predictive tasks. This research thus builds a foundation for accurate and interpretable systems, adaptable to various domains needing robust classification, fulfilling the core intent of advancing predictive methodologies through systematic design.

Despite these achievements, certain constraints exist. The study struggles with smaller classes like df, where low sample size limits model learning, as seen in F1-scores around 40%. Testing is confined to the HAM10000 dataset, which may not cover all real-world variations. Computational resources also restrict extensive hyperparameter tuning, potentially missing optimal settings. Future work could explore larger datasets and advanced balancing techniques to address these gaps.

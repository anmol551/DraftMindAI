1. BACKGROUND
----------------------------------------
The Rise of Automated Talent Acquisition

Automated talent acquisition systems combine information extraction and document classification to handle hiring processes more effectively. As job applications grow the recruitment industry faces challenges with employers often dealing with over 250 applications per job posting according to surveys by Pal (2022). This shift from manual methods highlights the need for tools that process resumes efficiently using natural language processing and machine learning.

Advancements in Resume Classification

Over time the field has progressed from basic rule-based classifiers to advanced hybrid models that blend traditional techniques with modern deep learning. For example early work with TF-IDF and Naive Bayes offered simple yet effective baselines as seen in studies like Ali et al. (2022) which achieved high accuracy on resume datasets. Meanwhile transformer models such as BERT have brought improvements in understanding resume content with Liang and Lin (2023) demonstrating enhanced performance in skill extraction tasks.

The Drive for Modern Solutions

In today's fast-changing job market researching automated systems is essential to tackle issues like bias and data inconsistencies. By focusing on integrated approaches this work helps fill gaps in existing methods promoting more reliable tools for recruitment as explored in Retyk et al. (2023). Such efforts pave the way for fairer and more adaptable hiring practices.

2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
----------------------------------------
Despite significant advancements in automated talent acquisition systems, a critical challenge persists in the fragmented approach to resume classification and skill extraction, where these processes are typically treated as isolated tasks rather than integrated components of a unified pipeline (Retyk et al., 2023). This separation hinders the development of efficient, end-to-end systems capable of handling the semantic complexity of modern resumes, leading to suboptimal performance in real-world recruitment scenarios. For instance, in high-volume hiring environments, such as corporate HR departments processing thousands of applications, disjointed methods result in delayed decision-making, increased error rates, and perpetuation of biases embedded in historical data, as evidenced by models that fail to adapt to evolving labor-market vocabularies (Vasquez-Rodriguez, 2024). Existing studies, such as those by Ali et al. (2022), have demonstrated the effectiveness of TF-IDF features combined with Multinomial Naive Bayes for fast classification, achieving over 96% accuracy on small datasets, yet these approaches overlook class imbalance issues like minority job category underrepresentation. Similarly, transformer-based models like DistilBERT, as explored by Zhang et al. (2022), enhance contextual embeddings for skill extraction but neglect systematic comparisons with traditional methods, limiting hybrid architectures that could leverage Bi-LSTM for sequence labeling alongside preprocessing steps such as SMOTE oversampling to address imbalances. This gap in integrating TF-IDF, Naive Bayes, Bi-LSTM, and DistilBERT within a cohesive framework—while incorporating imbalance correction—constrains the creation of robust, deployment-ready solutions, underscoring the need for research that bridges these methodologies to improve accuracy, fairness, and efficiency in talent acquisition systems. Research Question
The primary focus of this study is to explore key aspects of NLP techniques in the context of automated resume classification.

1. How does advanced NLP preprocessing impact the accuracy of automated resume classification?  
2. How do deep learning models compare to traditional methods for skill-based resume categorisation?

Objectives
The objectives outline the specific steps and goals that will be pursued to address the research questions.

1. To investigate existing research on resume classification and information extraction using NLP.  
2. To design and implement a preprocessing pipeline for parsing and cleaning PDF resumes.  
3. To extract and prioritise key entities such as skills and education using Named Entity Recognition.  
4. To apply TF-IDF with n-grams for feature representation of resumes.  
5. To implement and compare Multinomial Naive Bayes, Bi-LSTM, and DistilBERT models for resume classification.  
6. To critically evaluate model performance using standard classification metrics.

5. SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
----------------------------------------
Scope of the Research

The scope of this research encompasses the development and evaluation of an automated resume classification system aimed at streamlining talent acquisition processes. Specifically, it investigates the application of natural language processing (NLP) and machine learning (ML) techniques to classify resumes into predefined categories based on textual content, thereby addressing the inefficiencies in manual screening amid growing application volumes (Ali et al., 2022). The study utilizes a publicly available resume dataset to preprocess and analyze textual data, employing feature extraction methods to enhance classification accuracy. It focuses on general NLP pipelines for text handling, including preprocessing steps such as tokenization and feature engineering, while integrating imbalance handling strategies to manage skewed data distributions. This research is confined to the context of English-language resumes from a specific dataset, emphasizing the integration of classical and advanced ML approaches for predictive modeling in recruitment. By doing so, it seeks to provide insights into improving decision support systems in human resources, highlighting the potential for scalable automation in hiring workflows (Pal, 2022). Overall, the scope is limited to exploratory analysis and model training on a single dataset, without extending to real-time deployment or multi-language applications.

Limitations of the Study

The limitations of this study primarily stem from data-related challenges, such as the inherent imbalance in class distributions, which can skew model performance and lead to overfitting on dominant categories, as commonly observed in imbalanced datasets (Zhang et al., 2022). Additionally, trust issues arise from the general reliability and accuracy of ML models in NLP tasks, where biases in training data may perpetuate demographic or occupational prejudices, affecting the fairness and generalizability of outcomes (Vasquez-Rodriguez, 2024). For instance, the dataset's reliance on historical recruitment data introduces potential inaccuracies due to evolving labor market vocabularies, making it difficult to ensure consistent model performance across dynamic job sectors. Market limitations further complicate the research, as rapid changes in industry trends and skill requirements can render models obsolete, limiting their applicability in real-world settings where new job roles emerge frequently (Retyk et al., 2023). In terms of overall model accuracy, the study faces challenges related to the variability in text quality and annotation inconsistencies, which can undermine predictive reliability. As an opinion, possible ways to handle these limitations include adopting more robust data augmentation techniques to address imbalances, implementing bias detection algorithms during training to enhance trust, and regularly updating datasets with current market data to improve accuracy—though these approaches would require additional resources and validation. Despite these efforts, the study's scope does not mitigate all constraints, as external factors like computational resources and evaluation inconsistencies across literature persist, potentially impacting the broader applicability of the findings (Akkasi, 2024). In summary, while the research provides valuable insights, its limitations underscore the need for cautious interpretation in practical contexts.

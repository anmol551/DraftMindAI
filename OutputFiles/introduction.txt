1. BACKGROUND
----------------------------------------
Credit risk assessment forms a cornerstone of financial technology where banks evaluate borrowers' ability to repay loans. Traditional methods like FICO scores often fall short in predicting defaults accurately. For example Jagtiani and Lemieux (2019) reported a correlation drop from 80% in 2007 to 35% in 2014-2015 indicating growing inaccuracies that leave lenders exposed to higher losses and borrowers facing unfair rejections.

Machine learning and deep learning step in to tackle these issues by creating advanced data-driven tools for credit evaluation. Mhlanga (2021) shows how machine learning uses alternative data to reach underbanked groups reducing risks. Key advancements include ensemble methods that boost prediction accuracy as noted in studies by Bao et al. (2019) and Dumitrescu et al. (2022). These approaches combine various techniques to handle complex financial data more effectively.

Currently this research matters as it fills gaps in interpretable models and integrated risk frameworks. Leo et al. (2019) point out delays in applying these technologies fully in banking. This study advances the field by exploring unified methods for better loan decisions fostering fairer outcomes in financial services.

2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
----------------------------------------
In the domain of credit risk assessment and loan approval prediction, a persistent challenge lies in the fragmented treatment of regression-based risk scoring and binary classification for loan decisions, which hinders the development of cohesive, interpretable predictive frameworks (Dumitrescu et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2021). This separation often results in models that excel in individual tasks but fail to deliver unified outputs, compromising the reliability of financial predictions and exacerbating issues such as algorithmic bias and regulatory non-compliance in banking institutions. For instance, as Jagtiani and Lemieux (2019) demonstrate, the declining correlation of traditional FICO scores with actual loan performance underscores how disjointed approaches contribute to inaccurate lending decisions, affecting underbanked populations and increasing financial instability (Mhlanga, 2021; Raji et al., 2020).

Existing attempts, such as those by Bao et al. (2019) employing two-stage ensemble models, have addressed aspects of credit scoring but fall short in integrating both regression and classification simultaneously, thereby neglecting essential preprocessing steps like domain-informed feature engineering—such as constructing composite indicators for income stability and debt stress—which could enhance model performance (Alam et al., 2020; Sheikh et al., 2020). Furthermore, the application of attention-based tabular deep learning architectures remains underexplored, limiting interpretability in complex financial data analysis (Taye, 2023). This gap necessitates a novel approach that unifies these elements, leveraging tailored preprocessing and advanced models to bridge the divide between predictive accuracy and ethical, compliant decision-making, ultimately fostering more equitable and effective loan approval processes. 4. SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
----------------------------------------
The scope of this research encompasses the application of machine learning techniques to analyze and predict credit risk for loan approval decisions, utilizing a synthetic financial dataset to explore patterns in borrower characteristics and financial behaviors. Specifically, the study involves comprehensive data exploration, preprocessing, feature engineering, and predictive modeling to identify key factors influencing loan outcomes, thereby contributing to more informed lending practices in the financial sector. By examining variables such as income stability, debt ratios, and historical payment behaviors, the research aims to enhance understanding of risk assessment processes, addressing the limitations of traditional methods like FICO scores that often fail to capture nuanced data-driven insights (Jagtiani and Lemieux, 2019). This approach seeks to bridge gaps in credit evaluation by focusing on interpretable and accurate predictions, while emphasizing the importance of ethical considerations in model development to mitigate biases and improve decision-making reliability.

However, several limitations constrain the research's generalizability and effectiveness. Data-related challenges include the use of synthetic datasets, which may not fully replicate real-world variability, potentially leading to inaccuracies in reflecting actual market conditions and introducing biases from imbalanced classes, as noted in studies highlighting the prevalence of skewed financial data (Sheikh et al., 2020). Trust issues arise from the inherent opacity of machine learning models, where reliability and accuracy can be compromised by factors such as overfitting to limited data or sensitivity to noisy inputs, thereby affecting the trustworthiness of predictions in high-stakes financial contexts. Additionally, market limitations, such as regulatory compliance and ethical risks like algorithmic bias against underrepresented groups, further hinder widespread adoption, as these factors can undermine model performance in diverse real-world applications (Raji et al., 2020). Overall, these constraints underscore the challenges in achieving robust, unbiased outcomes from data-driven financial tools.

In the researcher's opinion, these limitations could be addressed through strategies such as incorporating more diverse and representative datasets to enhance data quality and reduce bias, while applying advanced techniques for model interpretability to bolster trust and reliability (Sharif et al., 2020). For instance, emphasizing feature selection and validation processes might improve accuracy by minimizing the impact of irrelevant variables, and integrating ethical frameworks could help navigate market constraints by ensuring compliance with regulations. By adopting these approaches, the research could achieve greater robustness, though their implementation would require careful balancing to maintain model efficacy without overcomplicating the analysis.

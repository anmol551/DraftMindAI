1. BACKGROUND
----------------------------------------
Background

Skin conditions affect millions globally with eczema being a common yet challenging issue to diagnose accurately. A survey by the National Eczema Association revealed that around 10% of the population in developed countries experience this condition often facing delayed or incorrect diagnosis due to overlapping symptoms with other skin issues. Such delays can worsen patient outcomes impacting quality of life. The complexity lies in distinguishing subtle visual cues in clinical images which often vary across individuals and settings creating a pressing need for better diagnostic tools.

Advancements in machine learning and deep learning offer promising solutions to tackle these challenges. These technologies can analyse medical images with remarkable precision helping to identify patterns that might be missed by the human eye. Research by Sharif et al. (2020) shows how convolutional neural networks excel in classifying skin conditions from images paving the way for improved diagnostic support. Their ability to adapt through transfer learning ensures better performance even with limited data.

Exploring this area now holds significant value as healthcare systems seek efficient ways to support clinicians. This study aims to bridge existing gaps by focusing on eczema-specific classification using publicly available image datasets. By building on current methods it seeks to refine diagnostic accuracy and address issues like dataset bias or class imbalance. This work contributes to creating reliable tools that could assist in timely and accurate identification of eczema ultimately benefiting patient care across diverse clinical environments.

2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
----------------------------------------
Problem Statement

In the domain of medical image analysis, deep learning has shown remarkable potential, particularly in dermatology for classifying skin conditions using dermoscopic images. However, a critical challenge persists in developing robust frameworks for binary classification of eczema versus non-eczema conditions, as existing approaches often fail to address dataset bias, class imbalance, and generalisation across diverse clinical settings (Jeong et al., 2022; Maron et al., 2021). This issue significantly impacts real-world applications, where misclassification of eczema can delay critical interventions, exacerbate patient outcomes, and burden healthcare systems, especially in resource-constrained environments. Previous efforts, while leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and hybrid architectures, frequently rely on small, institutionally biased datasets or broad multiclass frameworks that overlook eczema-specific textural nuances, thus limiting reproducibility and clinical utility (Rasheed et al., 2022; Hammad et al., 2023). Moreover, the lack of systematic preprocessing pipelines—such as denoising, contrast enhancement via CLAHE, and sharpening—tailored to eczema’s subtle visual cues further hinders model performance. The integration of structured preprocessing with transfer learning models like EfficientNetB0 and ResNet50, alongside strategies to mitigate class imbalance through stratified sampling, remains underexplored. This gap underscores the pressing need for a dedicated binary classification framework that prioritizes eczema detection, ensuring generalisation and clinical relevance. The present research seeks to address this deficiency by focusing on a comprehensive approach that combines targeted preprocessing and balanced training to enhance diagnostic accuracy, thereby contributing to improved dermatological care and decision-making in diverse clinical contexts.

3. SIGNIFICANCE
----------------------------------------
Significance of the Research Study

This research holds substantial significance in advancing the application of machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) in the field of dermatology, particularly for the automated classification of eczema using dermoscopic images. By integrating a clinically motivated multi-stage preprocessing pipeline with a two-phase transfer learning approach, this study contributes to the theoretical understanding of how tailored image processing and model fine-tuning can enhance diagnostic precision for inflammatory skin conditions. The unique methodological framework, which includes specialized denoising, contrast enhancement, and strategic data balancing, addresses critical challenges in handling imbalanced datasets and noisy medical imagery, thereby offering a robust blueprint for future research in medical image analysis. This work not only pushes the boundaries of DL architectures like ResNet50 and EfficientNetB0 in a controlled experimental setup but also provides insights into optimizing pretrained models for domain-specific tasks, as supported by prior studies on transfer learning in medical imaging (Sharif et al., 2020).

Beyond academic contributions, this research has practical implications for healthcare practitioners and institutions by facilitating early and accurate detection of eczema, a condition often misdiagnosed due to its visual similarity with other skin disorders. The deployment of the model within a lightweight Flask web application underscores its real-world applicability, enabling accessible diagnostic support for dermatologists, especially in resource-constrained settings. This tool can benefit clinicians by reducing diagnostic workload and improving screening efficiency, while also serving as an educational resource for medical students learning to interpret dermoscopic features. The broader societal impact lies in enhancing patient outcomes through timely intervention, particularly for underserved populations with limited access to specialized care, aligning with global health equity goals highlighted in recent literature (Sharif et al., 2020).

Furthermore, this study’s significance extends to policymakers and technology developers in the healthcare sector, providing a foundation for integrating AI-driven tools into clinical workflows. By demonstrating a deployable end-to-end diagnostic solution, it informs the development of scalable telemedicine platforms and regulatory frameworks for AI in medicine. For researchers and students in ML and DL, this work offers a replicable framework for tackling class imbalance and domain adaptation challenges in other medical imaging contexts. Ultimately, the research bridges the gap between theoretical innovation and practical utility, fostering advancements that can transform dermatological care and inspire cross-disciplinary collaboration in health informatics (Sharif et al., 2020).

4. SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
----------------------------------------
Scope and Limitations of the Research

This research focuses on developing a deep learning framework for the binary classification of eczema versus other dermatological conditions using dermoscopic images. The primary objective is to address the critical need for automated and accurate identification of eczema, an under-represented condition in many public repositories, by leveraging advanced image processing techniques and transfer learning. The study aims to tackle class imbalance through strategic sampling methods and augmentation, ensuring a balanced representation during training. By focusing on dermoscopic data, the research seeks to contribute to the field of medical image analysis, particularly in dermatology, where early and precise diagnosis can significantly impact patient outcomes (Jeong et al., 2022). The scope encompasses the exploration of robust preprocessing methods to enhance image quality and reduce noise, alongside the application of established architectures to ensure effective feature extraction for distinguishing subtle textural differences inherent to eczema (Rasheed et al., 2022).

However, several limitations are observed in this study, primarily stemming from data-related constraints and broader systemic challenges. The reliance on publicly available datasets introduces potential biases, as these repositories are often curated with a focus on cancerous conditions rather than inflammatory diseases like eczema, leading to skewed distributions and limited generalizability (Maron et al., 2021). Trust issues also arise due to the lack of histopathological ground truth in many images, which affects the reliability of labels and, consequently, the overall accuracy of automated systems (Hammad et al., 2023). Furthermore, market limitations, such as the scarcity of diverse, eczema-specific datasets and the high cost of acquiring clinical data across varied demographics, hinder the scalability of such frameworks in real-world settings (Alshahrani et al., 2024). These factors collectively pose challenges to the robustness and applicability of the research outcomes across different clinical environments.

To address these limitations, potential strategies could include collaborative efforts to curate more balanced and diverse datasets that better represent inflammatory conditions, possibly through partnerships with dermatological institutions. Enhancing data quality by integrating segmentation techniques to isolate relevant features and reduce confounders could also improve reliability, as suggested by prior studies (Maron et al., 2021). Additionally, incorporating interpretability tools to build trust among clinicians by highlighting decision-making processes might mitigate concerns regarding system dependability (Singh et al., 2022). While these approaches are speculative, they offer a pathway to refine the applicability of such frameworks. Despite these constraints, this research lays a foundation for advancing automated dermatological diagnostics, acknowledging the need for continuous improvement in data diversity and system validation to ensure broader clinical relevance (Venugopal et al., 2023).

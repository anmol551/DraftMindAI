Literature Review
Foundations of Automated Talent Acquisition Systems and Resume Classification Frameworks
Automated talent-acquisition systems sit at the intersection of information extraction, document classification and decision support; recent literature shows a clear transition from heuristic, rule-heavy pipelines to approaches that combine established feature-based classifiers with contextual embeddings from transformers. Early practical systems still rely on TF–IDF vectorisation and frequency-based classifiers because they are fast, interpretable and cheap to run; several empirical studies of resume-classification pipelines report robust baseline performance using TF–IDF + Multinomial Naive Bayes or SVM (Ali et al., 2022; Pal, 2022). Ali et al. (2022) illustrate this point on a curated resume corpus where binary / TF–IDF features plus Naive Bayes produced competitive classification accuracy (reported as the principal baseline across many engineering implementations). Those methods remain attractive for low-resource settings and for initial filtering stages because they require tens to hundreds — not millions — of labelled examples and yield transparent feature weights that HR teams can inspect.
However, the last three years of empirical work make two things apparent. First, domain adaptation matters: models pre-trained or fine-tuned on job-market text consistently beat out generic off-the-shelf pipelines. Zhang et al.’s SkillSpan work (Zhang et al., 2022) introduced a domain-specific dataset (14.5K sentences; ~12.5K annotated spans) and demonstrated that BERT variants adapted to job postings (JobBERT / JobSpanBERT) improved span F1 over untuned BERT baselines (BERT: total span F1 ≈ 57.7%; jobSpanBERT: ≈ 58.9%) — a non-trivial gain for downstream matching and normalization. Second, hybrid and ensemble architectures bridge the gap between classic and modern approaches: Akkasi (2024) reports an ensemble combining CNNs, bi-LSTMs, CRF and transformer modules that reached 72% F-score for technical skills and 67% for non-technical skills on a sizeable annotated job-description corpus, outperforming CRF and simpler deep models by margins of 6–29% depending on the skill type. These results indicate that, for recruitment tasks, the sequence-modelling strengths of transformers and the structural inductive biases of sequence labellers both contribute measurable benefits.
From a systems perspective, there is therefore a pragmatic stratification: TF–IDF + classical classifiers remain compelling for initial triage (low latency, high interpretability); transformer embeddings (BERT, DistilBERT, S-BERT) are best used when semantic nuance changes ranking decisions; and ensembles or fine-tuned LLMs add value when extraction precision is essential — for example, in normalising skills or identifying multi-token spans. Practical evaluations also stress dataset scale and annotation detail: SkillSpan’s ~14.5K sentences enabled robust comparisons across fine-tuning regimes (Zhang et al., 2022), while smaller production studies (e.g. Deshmukh & Raut, 2024) use a few hundred resumes and report much faster per-resume throughput (0.233s/resume) but with lower generalisability. Finally, strictly rule-based parsers still report strong micro-metrics for well-structured fields (Musleh, 2024 reported item-level F1 ≈ 0.825; section-level F1 ≈ 0.833), reminding researchers that rules + regex remain valuable for high-precision extraction of constrained fields (e.g. email, phone, degree) and are often used in hybrid pipelines.
Natural Language Processing Techniques for Resume Parsing and Structured Information Extraction
The resume parsing subfield has matured into two clear methodological families: (1) pattern-oriented rule systems targeted at high-precision extraction of predictable fields, and (2) learned sequence-labelling and span-extraction models that handle open-ended skill and experience text. Rule-based systems retain advantages where the target token set is small and annotation cost is prohibitive — Musleh (2024) demonstrates that a rule pipeline can reach >96% precision on contact and demographic fields — but such systems struggle with implicit, paraphrased or domain-specific skills.
Sequence labelling approaches — CRFs, Bi-LSTMs and now transformers — are preferred for skill and experience extraction because they capture context and token boundaries. The SkillSpan benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2022) show how span-focused objectives and domain pretraining help: domain-adapted JobBERT variants improve span F1 over generic BERT by a few percentage points, and models that use span-aware losses (SpanBERT variants) consistently score better at extracting longer multi-token skills. This is not only an academic increment: in recruitment pipelines, correctly identifying a 2–4 token skill (e.g. “continuous integration”, “Docker containerisation”) can change downstream matching and ranking decisions.
Transformer-based embeddings and sentence encoders (S-BERT, DistilBERT) have been adopted for two purposes: (a) feature extraction for classification/ranking (embedding resumes and job descriptions for cosine similarity or nDCG ranking), and (b) direct token-level labelling. Deshmukh & Raut (2024) show S-BERT embeddings enable a screening system that processes ~223 PDFs at ~0.233s/resume and reports 90% accuracy on their test set — a useful demonstration of embedding-based ranking at small scale. More recent work explores fine-tuning large language models directly for extraction tasks: Herandi et al. (2024) (Skill-LLM) fine-tuned an 8B-parameter LLaMA-3 model and achieved an overall span F1 of 64.8% on SkillSpan (skills: 54.3%; knowledge: 74.2%), outperforming several transformer baselines. These results are important because they show that, for span-extraction tasks with rich annotation, fine-tuned LLMs can surpass classical transformer fine-tuning — but at higher computational cost.
Crucially, head-to-head comparisons reveal trade-offs rather than absolute winners. Controlled studies (Garrido-Merchán et al., 2023; Bokolo & Liu, 2024) indicate transformers typically outperform TF–IDF+classical models on semantically complex datasets, but traditional models can match or exceed transformer performance in simpler, low-variance tasks or when labelled data are sparse. Therefore, in practical ATS design, a layered approach is analytically justified: use rule extraction for high-precision fields, TF–IDF models for initial triage where interpretability and speed are priorities, and transformers (or fine-tuned LLMs) for skill extraction, matching and final ranking where context matters and compute is available. This alignment of method to problem complexity is the central lesson from recent work and guides the design choices for automating talent acquisition in real recruitment settings.
Machine Learning Approaches to Resume Categorisation and Predictive Talent Analytics
Work on automated resume analysis has increasingly reframed classification and recommendation as tightly coupled tasks: accurate label prediction (e.g. role, seniority, domain) is a precondition for reliable downstream matching and analytics (shortlisting, diversity metrics, turnover risk estimation). Research since 2021 therefore splits along two axes — lightweight pipelines optimised for throughput and explainability, and heavier models that trade cost for richer representations. Empirically, sequence-level encoders such as Bi-LSTM (often with a CRF decoder) still offer competitive performance for token-level labelling when training data are modest and annotations are precise; Liang et al. (2023) demonstrate an Attention-BiLSTM + BiLSTM-CNNs-CRF architecture that produces strong line-classification and entity-recognition performance on common résumé corpora and emphasise latency/performance trade-offs in production settings (Liang, 2023). These recurrent methods benefit from simplicity: they require fewer GPU resources than large transformers, and they are straightforward to augment with character-level CNNs to capture orthographic patterns in technical terms.
On the other hand, transformer-based encoders (BERT family and distilled variants) dominate when the task requires semantic generalisation — particularly for multi-token skills and paraphrased descriptions. SkillSpan’s benchmark work shows domain-adaptive pretraining and span-aware models meaningfully improve extraction of hard and soft skills: SKILLSPAN contains 14.5k annotated sentences and over 12.5k skill/knowledge spans, and Zhang et al. (2022) report that domain-adapted BERT variants and models optimised for long spans outperform generic BERT baselines on span extraction tasks (Zhang et al., 2022). The analytical implication is clear: for predictive talent analytics that depend on granular skill inventories (skill co-occurrence, gap analysis), contextualised embeddings matter.
Comparative evaluations emphasise trade-offs rather than simple superiority. Retyk et al. (2023) recast résumé parsing as hierarchical sequence labelling (line + token levels) and show transformer backbones improve F1 for entity extraction across several languages, but they also report resource-efficiency considerations that make Bi-LSTM variants preferable in low-latency deployments (Retyk et al., 2023). Akkasi (2024) presents an explainable transformer-ensemble for job-description parsing that achieved F1 ≈ 72% on technical skills and ≈ 67% on non-technical skills — improvements of several percentage points compared to CRF and simpler deep models — which illustrates the consistent, if sometimes modest, gains that transformers provide for skill tasks (Akkasi, 2024). Practically, therefore, research recommends a tiered architecture: fast, interpretable models (TF–IDF + Naive Bayes / SVM or Bi-LSTM) for initial triage; transformer-based modules for skill extraction and fine ranking where semantic nuance affects hiring decisions.
Domain-specific Text Encodings and Advanced Models for Skill Extraction and Semantic Matching
A persistent theme in recent literature is that generic pretraining does not always suffice for labour-market text. Domain-specific embeddings — obtained via continued pretraining on job postings, CVs or enterprise corpora — consistently yield uplift for skill extraction and matching. SkillSpan’s experiments with domain-adapted models show that continuous pretraining on job-posting corpora reduces errors on long, domain-specific spans (Zhang et al., 2022). These results are corroborated in applied studies that create “job-aware” encoders for matching: models fine-tuned or pre-adapted on in-domain corpora produce denser, more discriminative vectors for skills and responsibilities, improving nearest-neighbour matching and ranking.
At the model level, two families are prominent for skill extraction framed as NER: (1) Bi-LSTM + CRF and its attention-augmented variants; and (2) transformer-based token labellers (BERT-style fine-tuning, distilled variants). Bi-LSTM models remain strong baselines when annotations are limited and token order is informative (Liang, 2023). Transformer models (including DistilBERT) provide gains for ambiguous or paraphrased skill mentions and long spans: DistilBERT, as a compact transformer, often achieves most of BERT’s performance at a fraction of latency in many applied studies, making it a pragmatic choice where throughput matters (Adel et al., 2022). In the résumé domain, Kesim and Deliahmetoglu (2023) adapted multiple transformer variants to NER on annotated IT resumes and found RoBERTa/ELECTRA variants gave the best F1 scores for common fields, arguing that transformer diversity is useful when entity distributions vary by subdomain.
Method comparisons in the literature are instructive. Studies that control for dataset size typically find: (a) with small labelled sets (hundreds of documents), Bi-LSTM/CRF or rule-augmented pipelines can match or exceed transformers because overfitting is less severe and feature engineering helps; (b) with moderate to large annotated corpora (thousands of labelled spans), transformers and domain-adapted encoders generally outperform recurrent models on F1 and recall for multi-token skills (Zhang et al., 2022; Retyk et al., 2023). Where hybrid systems are evaluated, combining rule-based extraction for constrained fields (contact, dates, degree names — high precision) with transformer NER for skills (higher recall on paraphrases) yields the best practical balance (Musleh, 2024; Vásquez-Rodríguez, 2024).
Challenges and practical limitations recur across studies and deserve critical attention. First, annotation cost and label drift: labour-market vocabularies evolve rapidly, so expensive span annotations risk obsolescence; Zhang et al. (2022) address this by releasing large unlabeled corpora for continual pretraining. Second, evaluation inconsistency: many studies use proprietary datasets or different span definitions, which complicates head-to-head comparisons (Retyk et al., 2023). Third, fairness and bias: models trained on historical hiring data can reproduce occupational and demographic biases — a non-technical but decisive constraint for deployment that several authors note (Vásquez-Rodríguez, 2024). Finally, engineering trade-offs remain: computational cost, latency and maintenance often drive architecture choices as much as raw F1 improvements.
For automation that must serve real HR decisions, the evidence supports hybrid architectures that combine rule-based precision, Bi-LSTM robustness in low-data regimes, and transformer semantics where cost and annotated scale permit — a pragmatic alignment of method to business need rather than pursuit of a single “best” model.
Research Gap
Recent studies in Resume Classification and Skill Extraction have demonstrated notable improvements through transformer-based models such as DistilBERT and deep learning architectures including Bi-LSTM for Named Entity Recognition. However, several critical gaps remain. First, many existing systems treat Resume Classification and Skill Extraction as isolated tasks rather than components of an integrated Talent Acquisition pipeline. This separation limits the practical deployment of end-to-end recruitment automation models.
Second, traditional Machine Learning approaches such as TF-IDF with Multinomial Naive Bayes continue to be used for their efficiency and interpretability, yet they are rarely combined systematically with contextual transformer embeddings. Most studies either benchmark traditional models against transformers or fully replace them, without exploring hybrid architectures that leverage both statistical robustness and semantic depth.
Third, domain-specific text encodings remain under-optimised. While pre-trained transformer models capture contextual meaning, they are often fine-tuned on relatively small or generic datasets, leading to suboptimal performance on recruitment-specific vocabulary, multi-token skills, and ambiguous competency descriptions. Similarly, Bi-LSTM-based Named Entity Recognition models struggle with evolving skill taxonomies and sparse annotations.
Therefore, there is a need for an integrated framework that strategically combines TF-IDF, Multinomial Naive Bayes, DistilBERT, and Bi-LSTM within a unified Natural Language Processing architecture to enhance automated Talent Acquisition systems.

Research Gap
----------------------------------------
Despite notable progress in resume classification and skill extraction using transformer-based and deep learning models, several critical gaps remain unaddressed in the existing literature. First, most studies treat resume classification and named entity recognition as independent tasks, preventing the development of integrated, end-to-end talent acquisition pipelines. This separation limits practical deployment in real-world recruitment systems where both categorisation and skill extraction must operate in tandem. Second, comparisons between traditional machine learning approaches — particularly TF-IDF with Multinomial Naive Bayes — and transformer-based architectures such as DistilBERT are rarely conducted within a unified experimental framework. Studies either benchmark traditional models in isolation or fully replace them with transformers, overlooking hybrid strategies that combine statistical robustness with contextual semantic representations. Third, class imbalance — a pervasive characteristic of real-world resume datasets — is inadequately addressed. Existing work rarely applies systematic techniques such as SMOTE oversampling, class-weighted loss functions, or per-class decision threshold tuning within the same pipeline, leaving minority job category prediction largely underperformed. Fourth, while Bi-LSTM models are frequently used for sequence-level labelling in NER tasks, their application to multi-class document-level resume classification alongside transformers in a controlled comparative study is absent from most recent literature. Fifth, there is limited work on deploying integrated classification and entity extraction systems through lightweight web applications, translating research models into accessible HR tools. These gaps collectively justify an integrated framework that combines TF-IDF, Multinomial Naive Bayes, Bi-LSTM, and DistilBERT — augmented with NER-based skill extraction, class imbalance correction, and per-class threshold tuning — within a unified, deployment-ready pipeline for automated talent acquisition.

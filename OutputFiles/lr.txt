Literature Review
Deep learning in medical image analysis
Over the past five years the literature has consolidated the view that deep learning is mature enough to deliver clinical-grade performance in particular image tasks, while still suffering important generalisability and dataset-bias issues (Jeong et al., 2022). Systematic reviews show that transfer learning from ImageNet remains the pragmatic default for medical imaging because it reduces data requirements and accelerates convergence; however, Jeong et al. (2022) caution that many studies over-optimistically report performance on held-out subsets of the same source rather than on truly external clinical data, which exaggerates real-world performance. This critique is echoed in robustness benchmarks that explicitly evaluate confounding effects (Maron et al., 2021): segmentation to remove lesion-adjacent artefacts consistently reduces spurious reliance on non-lesional cues, but segmentation itself may change data distributions and introduce other pitfalls (Maron et al., 2021). For a framework that aims to distinguish eczema from other skin diseases, these two lessons are central — transfer learning offers immediate gains, but careful validation on external clinical sets and attention to confounders are necessary to avoid models that exploit peripheral signals (Jeong et al., 2022; Maron et al., 2021).
A second trend is the rise of transformer-based and hybrid architectures in medical imaging. Scoping reviews show that vision transformers and CNN–transformer hybrids can capture longer-range context in images (Khan et al., 2023), which could be useful for dermatological conditions that manifest as diffuse textural changes rather than focal lesions. Yet transformer methods typically demand more data or clever pretraining; several comparative studies therefore opt for computationally efficient CNN backbones (EfficientNet variants) and still report competitive results (Manole et al., 2024). For eczema, which often presents with variable morphological features and limited high-quality dermoscopic corpora, a hybrid strategy — CNN feature extractor plus lightweight attention module — may be the most pragmatic compromise (Khan et al., 2023; Manole et al., 2024).
Convolutional neural networks for skin disease classification
Classical CNNs remain the dominant architecture for dermoscopic classification tasks; the literature documents mature pipelines that pair standard backbones (ResNet, DenseNet, EfficientNet) with transfer learning and heavy augmentation (Alshahrani et al., 2024; Venugopal et al., 2023). Alshahrani et al. (2024) demonstrate that hybrid systems combining features from multiple CNNs often outperform single-model baselines on ISIC datasets, but the apparent gains are partly explained by ensembling over the same underlying biases in public datasets. Venugopal et al. (2023) and Manole et al. (2024) show that careful selection of EfficientNet variants can achieve excellent accuracy while keeping computational cost low, which matters for deployment in resource-limited clinical settings.
Focusing on eczema, a smaller but growing set of papers attempts direct classification. Rasheed et al. (2022) present a hybrid deep model for multiclass eczema categorisation trained on a purpose-built EIR dataset; the approach achieves promising internal metrics but relies on handcrafted features fused with CNN activations, illustrating that for skin conditions with subtle textural and colour variability, pure end-to-end CNNs sometimes underperform hybrid pipelines (Rasheed et al., 2022). Hammad et al. (2023) compared eczema vs psoriasis detection and reported improved accuracy using ensemble CNNs, yet both studies emphasise small class sizes and intra-class heterogeneity as limiting factors. Collectively these works point to two practical conclusions for eczema vs other conditions: (1) classical CNNs remain strong baselines and benefit greatly from ensembling/feature fusion, and (2) small-class regimes require targeted augmentation, class-balanced loss functions and external validation to avoid optimistic claims (Rasheed et al., 2022; Hammad et al., 2023).
A further methodological gap concerns uncertainty and explainability. SkiNet (Singh et al., 2022) shows that combining segmentation, Monte Carlo dropout and saliency methods substantially improves clinician trust and highlights failure modes; such mechanisms are important for eczema classification because false negatives (missed active dermatitis) or false positives (unnecessary referrals) have markedly different clinical consequences. SkiNet’s two-stage segmentation-then-classify pipeline also exemplifies how segmentation can improve downstream classification robustness — a theme echoed in Maron et al. (2021).
Dermoscopic imaging and its role in automated dermatological diagnosis
Dermatoscopic (dermoscopic) imaging remains the gold standard for many lesion analyses and is heavily used in datasets such as HAM10000 and ISIC (Jeong et al., 2022; Alshahrani et al., 2024). Studies using dermoscopy show higher diagnostic signal for pigmented lesions than for diffuse inflammatory conditions; eczema’s dermoscopic signatures are less well characterised and datasets with validated dermoscopy for eczema are scarce (Rasheed et al., 2022; Hammad et al., 2023). Consequently, many eczema studies work with clinical (macro) images rather than dermoscopy, which changes the model design space and generalisability expectations.
Benchmarking work and dataset papers (e.g. recent large curated collections and tooling papers) emphasise that dataset composition matters more than incremental model tweaks: larger, more diverse and well-labelled sets (including different skin tones and imaging devices) materially improve external validity (Kurtansky et al., 2024; Cassidy et al., 2022). Where dermoscopy is available, segmentation and lesion-ROI approaches tend to reduce confounders and improve cross-site performance (Maron et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2022). Still, dermoscopy may not always be practical for eczema screening in primary care — here portable, robust CNNs trained on clinical images and paired with uncertainty outputs (SkiNet-style) may be more clinically useful (Singh et al., 2022; Manole et al., 2024).
Recent literature (2021–2024) suggests a practical path for an eczema vs other condition classifier: start with strong CNN backbones using transfer learning (EfficientNet family), adopt segmentation where dermoscopy is available to reduce confounders, employ data-centric strategies (augmentation, class-balanced losses, external test sets), and include uncertainty/explainability to increase clinical acceptability. The studies reviewed indicate both the potential of AI in dermatology and the persistent gaps — especially data scarcity for inflammatory diseases like eczema and the need for careful external validation to avoid misleadingly optimistic performance claims (Jeong et al., 2022; Maron et al., 2021; Rasheed et al., 2022).
Binary classification for eczema versus other dermatological conditions
Binary (two-class) paradigms remain important when the clinical question is “eczema vs non-eczema” and when downstream triage decisions are required. However, recent literature shows a tension between the convenience of binary targets and the heterogeneity of “non-eczema” classes. Venugopal et al. (2023) demonstrate that architectures modified from EfficientNet can achieve strong malignant/benign or binary performance on large mixed dermoscopic sets, but they emphasise that assembling multiple datasets to create the binary labels can mix lesions with very different visual signatures, which inflates apparent performance when the validation split inadvertently preserves dataset-specific artefacts (Venugopal et al., 2023). Maron et al. (2021) similarly warn that confounding visual cues (rulers, ink marks, image borders) can drive apparently high binary accuracy unless segmentation or careful preprocessing is applied (Maron et al., 2021). This is highly relevant for eczema: eczema’s dermoscopic appearance overlaps with many inflammatory dermatoses and even non-inflammatory lesions, so binary eczema/non-eczema models trained on naively pooled datasets risk learning spurious correlates rather than disease-specific patterns (Maron et al., 2021; Venugopal et al., 2023).
Some studies have specifically targeted inflammatory disease discrimination. Hammad et al. (2023) developed a CNN pipeline aiming to distinguish eczema from psoriasis and other conditions on clinical images; while their reported metrics are encouraging, the paper highlights two consistent issues: small, often institutionally biased eczema subsets, and lack of histopathological ground truth for many inflammatory labels (Hammad et al., 2023). Practically, this means binary eczema classifiers need not only careful dataset curation but also explicit attention to confounders and external validation on independent dermatology clinics before deployment.
Transfer learning and CNN / modern architectures in dermatology
Transfer learning from ImageNet and the adoption of compact backbones (EfficientNet family, ResNet variants) remain dominant in dermoscopic work, and recent studies compare and adapt these backbones for clinical constraints. Kanchana et al. (2024) and Manole (2024) show that EfficientNet variants provide a favourable accuracy/resource trade-off for dermoscopic tasks when fine-tuned with task-relevant augmentations (Kanchana et al., 2024; Manole, 2024). Venugopal et al. (2023) extend this by modifying EfficientNet blocks to incorporate multi-scale features and report improvements in both binary and multiclass scenarios (Venugopal et al., 2023). Such studies suggest EfficientNet-based transfer learning will be a sensible starting point for an eczema classifier, especially where inference speed matters.
But the literature also reveals limits to straightforward transfer learning. Singh et al. (2022) show that segmentation followed by classification (a two-stage pipeline) can reduce uncertainty and improve reliability compared with single-stage transfer learning approaches (Singh et al., 2022). Similarly, Khan (2021) and others demonstrate that combining segmentation or localisation cues with transfer-learned classifiers often improves robustness on small, clinically realistic datasets (Khan, 2021). Emerging transformer-based models and hybrids (reviewed in Khan, 2023) have shown promise but often require larger datasets or careful regularisation; thus, while ViTs may offer long-term benefits, current pragmatic frameworks for eczema classification still favour carefully fine-tuned, attention-enhanced CNN backbones (Khan, 2021; Manole, 2024).
Handling class imbalance, augmentation and AI-assisted diagnosis
Class imbalance is a major practical barrier for eczema detection: eczema cases are under-represented in many public dermoscopic repositories, and disease subtypes (acute vs chronic eczema) are unevenly sampled. A clear pattern in the recent literature is the reliance on synthetic augmentation and GAN-based synthesis to expand minority classes. Innani et al. (2023) and Behara et al. (2023) demonstrate that GANs (Efficient-GAN, DCGAN variants) can improve segmentation and classification when synthetic images are used carefully, but both papers caution that naive GAN augmentation can create artefacts that models over-fit to (Innani et al., 2023; Behara et al., 2023). Goceri (2024) argues for hybrid loss and perceptual consistency terms to make GAN outputs more clinically plausible, an important consideration if GANs are to be used to bolster eczema class counts (Goceri, 2024).
Complementary to GANs, Shen et al. (2022) and Bozkurt (2023) show that systematic augmentation search (e.g. low-cost augment search; affine and photometric policies) often yields more reliable gains than blind oversampling, particularly when validated across ISIC/HAM-type splits (Shen et al., 2022; Bozkurt, 2023). Importantly, Efat et al. (2024) show ensemble methods combined with attention modules and tailored transfer learning can further stabilise performance across imbalanced splits, at the cost of increased model complexity (Efat et al., 2024). For eczema, the upshot is that augmentation should be conservative and validated for clinical plausibility; combining carefully searched geometric/colour augmentation with selective synthetic image generation and class-aware loss weighting appears to be the most defensible path forward.
On AI-assisted diagnosis and clinical translation, multiple papers (Singh et al., 2022; Venugopal et al., 2023) emphasise uncertainty estimation and explainability as prerequisites for deployment. SkiNet (Singh et al., 2022) demonstrates how uncertainty maps and saliency methods can reduce false certainty in borderline cases—an especially important property for eczema where misclassification could delay treatment or trigger inappropriate steroid prescribing. In short, the literature supports a framework that combines robust transfer learning (EfficientNet-family starting point), precise segmentation or localisation to reduce confounding, targeted augmentation plus class-aware training, and uncertainty/explainability layers to make predictions clinically interpretable for eczema vs other conditions.
Synthesis of the Literature and Research Gap
The reviewed literature demonstrates a clear and sustained growth in the application of deep learning techniques to dermatological image classification, particularly using convolutional neural networks and transfer learning. Recent studies consistently report strong performance when pre-trained CNN architectures, such as EfficientNet and ResNet variants, are fine-tuned on dermoscopic datasets. Segmentation-assisted pipelines, ensemble models and attention mechanisms have further improved classification robustness, while uncertainty estimation and explainability techniques have been increasingly recognised as important for clinical acceptance. Collectively, these trends indicate that deep learning has reached a level of technical maturity suitable for assisting dermatological diagnosis, especially in image-based screening contexts.
Despite these advances, several methodological limitations persist across the literature. A dominant concern is dataset bias, arising from the heavy reliance on a small number of public dermoscopic repositories that were originally curated for skin cancer research. As a result, models often exploit confounding artefacts or dataset-specific cues, leading to inflated performance estimates and poor generalisation to external clinical data. Class imbalance remains a particularly acute issue for inflammatory skin conditions such as eczema, which are under-represented compared to pigmented lesions. Although data augmentation and GAN-based synthesis have been proposed as mitigation strategies, existing studies highlight the risk of introducing unrealistic patterns or reinforcing spurious correlations when these techniques are applied without careful validation.
A further limitation is the lack of eczema-specific modelling in current research. Most dermatological classifiers adopt broad multiclass or generic binary formulations (e.g. benign vs malignant), with eczema either grouped into a heterogeneous “other” category or excluded entirely. Studies that explicitly target eczema often rely on small, institution-specific datasets and clinical images rather than dermoscopic imagery, limiting their reproducibility and diagnostic precision. Consequently, there is little consensus on optimal model architectures, preprocessing strategies, or evaluation protocols for eczema classification using dermoscopy.
These gaps directly motivate the need for the present study. There is a clear absence of robust, eczema-focused binary classification frameworks that leverage dermoscopic images while addressing class imbalance, confounding factors and generalisation concerns. By framing the task as a binary problem—eczema versus other dermatological conditions—this research aligns with realistic clinical decision-making and reduces unnecessary label complexity. The synthesis of transfer learning, carefully designed preprocessing, and class-aware training strategies offers a principled path toward improving diagnostic reliability. Research Gap
----------------------------------------
Despite considerable advances in deep learning for dermatological image classification, a notable gap persists in the development of dedicated binary frameworks for distinguishing eczema from other dermatological conditions using publicly available dermoscopic datasets. The majority of existing studies adopt broad multiclass formulations or focus on skin cancer detection, with eczema either grouped within heterogeneous non-specific categories or excluded altogether. Studies that explicitly address eczema classification frequently rely on small, institutionally curated datasets, handcrafted feature extraction pipelines, or limited CNN architectures such as AlexNet and standalone ResNet without fine-tuning strategies, thereby constraining reproducibility and clinical applicability (Rasheed et al., 2022; Hammad et al., 2023).

A particularly underexplored area is the structured use of multi-stage preprocessing pipelines — incorporating denoising, CLAHE-based contrast enhancement, and image sharpening — prior to training transfer learning models on dermoscopic data. Existing works rarely integrate all three preprocessing components in a systematic pipeline tailored to eczema's subtle textural and erythematous signatures. Similarly, while class imbalance correction through stratified downsampling followed by minority class upsampling to achieve perfect training balance has been theoretically advocated, few studies implement this combined two-stage balancing strategy alongside progressive transfer learning fine-tuning for eczema-specific binary classification.

The comparative evaluation of multiple deep learning architectures — including custom CNNs as a baseline alongside EfficientNetB0 and ResNet50 with two-phase fine-tuning — under a unified preprocessing and data balancing pipeline is absent from the eczema classification literature. Furthermore, the deployment of such frameworks within lightweight Flask-based clinical web applications for real-time binary inference has not been adequately addressed. The present research addresses these gaps by implementing a structured, end-to-end binary classification pipeline combining systematic preprocessing, balanced training, fine-tuned transfer learning, and a deployable web interface, thereby contributing a reproducible and clinically oriented framework for eczema versus non-eczema discrimination.

Literature Review
The issue of credit risk in loan approval is critical for financial institutions aiming to minimize potential losses. D Mhlanga (2021) highlights the challenges faced by underbanked individuals who often lack traditional forms of collateral or identification necessary for loan approval. The study identifies how machine learning and artificial intelligence can mitigate these challenges by utilising alternative data sources, allowing lenders to conduct thorough credit risk assessments. This integration aims to address information asymmetry and reduce adverse selection. 
Recent literature sheds light on the increasing relevance of machine learning techniques in banking risk management, specifically in loan approval contexts. M Leo et al. (2019) point out that despite the extensive research in the application of machine learning, the traditional methods for assessing credit risk may not suffice in the face of emerging challenges. The review indicates that while significant strides have been made in utilising machine learning for credit scoring, there remains a scarcity of in-depth exploration of certain banking risks that could benefit from machine learning insights. The analysis encourages further research in areas that have been overlooked, thereby pushing the boundaries of existing banking practices. 
Integration of unsupervised learning with supervised learning strategies is a growing area of interest that could reshape credit risk assessment methodologies in the financial sector. In the work of W Bao et al. (2019), the authors propose combining these two learning paradigms in distinct stages to improve the effectiveness of credit scoring models. Their findings indicate that both the consensus stage and dataset clustering contribute positively to enhancing model performance. 
Role of Machine Learning in Loan Approval
Machine learning is showing significant promise in transforming loan approval processes by enhancing credit risk assessments. Research indicates that traditional credit scoring reliant on FICO scores can be ineffective, particularly for loans in recent years. Jagtiani and Lemieux (2019) highlight a stark decline in the correlation between the LendingClub rating grades and FICO scores, dropping from approximately 80% in 2007 to around 35% in 2014 and 2015. This shift suggests that as alternative data sources gain traction among fintech lenders, these nontraditional metrics could provide more accurate assessments of borrower risk. 
Further evidence from Mhlanga (2021) supports the argument that machine learning applications play an essential role in addressing credit risk assessment, especially in emerging markets where conventional criteria can inadvertently exclude underbanked individuals. The utilisation of machine learning and artificial intelligence offers clear advantages in utilising alternative data sources. By mitigating issues like information asymmetry and adverse selection, banks can improve their risk assessment methodologies. This access to credit not only empowers financially excluded households but also enhances lenders' ability to analyse customer behaviour patterns effectively. 
The financial services sector is increasingly recognising the importance of machine learning techniques for enhancing credit scoring models. Dumitrescu et al. (2022) addressed that ensemble methods, particularly random forests, outperform traditional logistic regression models in classification tasks. However, they also note the limitations of these methods in terms of interpretability that remains vital for regulatory compliance. Their proposal of penalised logistic tree regression (PLTR) that combines the strengths of decision trees and logistic regression, stands out as a significant advancement. This balance of accuracy and interpretability is critical in ensuring that machine learning tools lead to effective and compliant credit assessments.
Implications of Imbalanced Data on Loan Approval Decisions
Imbalanced data significantly influences loan approval decisions by impacting the performance of credit risk prediction models. Research by Namvar et al. (2018) highlights that class imbalance is particularly detrimental in peer-to-peer lending, where standard models often neglect the complexities associated with imbalanced datasets. The study conducted an empirical comparison of classifiers and resampling techniques, revealing that the G-mean measure proved more effective than traditional metrics. By focusing on combinations of random forest with random under-sampling, they showed a marked improvement in predicting loan repayment likelihood. This underlines the critical role of appropriate resampling techniques in achieving reliable financial assessments.
Evidence from Alam et al. (2020) supports the assertion that accurately predicting credit default is inherently tied to addressing the challenges of imbalanced data. Their study utilised Min-Max normalisation alongside resampling methods to improve performance across various classification models. Different datasets revealed accuracy improvements, particularly with the proposed methodologies. The use of one-way Analysis of Variance offered a significant insight into whether different machine learning techniques and resampling strategies led to varying results.
The implications of these findings lead to a clear understanding that imbalanced data dramatically affects loan approval outcomes. Sheikh et al. (2020) puts focus on the importance of incorporating a wider array of attributes, such as the borrower's demographics, into the prediction model that can improve the accuracy of risk assessment. The logistic regression model used in their study showed varying performance based on the range of variables analysed. This suggests that relying solely on financial status without accounting for other factors may skew assessments and lead to profit losses for banks.

Ensemble Learning
Key point: The use of ensemble learning for credit risk assessment can significantly enhance prediction accuracy, as shown in the work of M Papouskova and P Hajek (2019). Their proposed two-stage credit risk model integrates class-imbalanced ensemble learning to predict the probability of default (PD) and uses regression ensemble methods for predicting exposure at default (EAD). The importance of advanced modelling techniques in credit risk cannot be overstated, as it aims to improve both predictive accuracy and economic performance compared to simpler models.

By using ensemble methods, lenders can achieve a finer analysis of various risk factors associated with loans. This aligns well with the traditional challenges observed in estimating PD, LGD, and EAD separately. The proposed methodology not only enhances prediction accuracy but also provides a clearer understanding of borrower risk profiles, essential for financial institutions aiming to minimize risk.
Concluding statement: Therefore, the adoption of ensemble learning methods could yield significant benefits for financial decision-making, particularly within risk assessment, reinforcing the need to continuously explore innovative modelling strategies.

Specifically, the study asserts that SVM delivers superior performance in regression tasks while XGBoost and RF are efficient and effective for classification tasks. This indicates that specific machine learning techniques can be aligned with the desired outcomes of financial data analysis.

Such comparative analyses provide valuable frameworks for optimising financial risk assessments and underline the significance of model selection informed by specific data characteristics.
Ethical Considerations in Loan Approval Models
One crucial aspect of ethical considerations in machine learning models is the potential for bias in financial risk assessments. Raji et al. (2020) explore how algorithms can inadvertently privilege certain groups while penalising others, especially in sensitive applications like loan approvals. They puts focus on the impact of biased data on machine learning outputs that can exacerbate existing inequalities. Recognising the ethical dimensions involved may lead to more equitable loan approval processes that consider the needs and circumstances of all applicants.
The design of the systems used for analysing financial data must account for the ethical dilemmas posed by biased outputs. Kasneci et al. (2023) suggest that training algorithms on imbalanced datasets can lead to skewed risk assessments that may disproportionately affect marginalised populations. This underscores the need for a methodological framework that prioritises fairness and equity in the training of models.
Trust in machine learning models is also a significant ethical consideration, particularly in user acceptance of automated systems. Baiano (2020) discusses how consumer trust varies based on individual characteristics, pointing out that ethical implications strongly influence users’ willingness to accept algorithms in decision-making processes. In the context of loan approvals, this suggests that models should be transparent and explainable. 
Regulatory Framework for Loan Approval Using Machine Learning
The use of machine learning (ML) in the assessment of credit risk and loan approvals showed the need for a strong regulatory framework to ensure ethical and effective outcomes. Research shows that while ML can enhance credit scoring and fraud detection, it is essential for financial institutions to understand the influence of data quality and algorithms involved in credit risk evaluation. Bhatore et al. (2020) highlight the importance of credit scoring systems and continuous monitoring to mitigate non-performing assets (NPAs). They conducted a systematic literature review of 136 studies, revealing that ensemble methods and hybrid models, particularly those incorporating neural networks and support vector machines, have garnered significant attention for improving credit risk predictions.
Upon reviewing these findings, it becomes evident that banks and financial institutions face a dual challenge: they must use advanced ML methodologies while ensuring compliance with ethical standards. Leo et al. (2019) puts focus on the fact that there remains a gap between the current capabilities of ML in risk management and the failures to fully implement these tools in practice. This disconnect raises concerns about the adequacy of current regulations in guiding ML applications in financial risk assessment. The authors note that various risks—including credit, market, and operational risks—can be better addressed through machine learning. 
In addressing these challenges, stakeholder collaboration is essential for refining the regulatory framework surrounding ML in lending practices. Mhlanga (2021) discusses how underbanked populations lack traditional identification methods, thus complicating their access to loans. The study advocates for using alternative data sources, a move that can potentially bridge the information asymmetry gap, but also warns of the risks associated with such practices. The insights provided suggest that while ML can improve credit evaluations, regulatory bodies must ensure that institutions do not misuse alternative data sources that could worsen disparities rather than resolve them.
Future Directions for Loan Approval in Machine Learning
One significant aspect of enhancing loan approval processes using machine learning involves the integration of ensemble and hybrid models. According to Bhatore et al. (2020), these models that combine different machine learning techniques, particularly neural networks and support vector machines, show improved performance in predicting credit risk. The current trends highlight that such models can not only improve accuracy but also help in minimising non-performing assets. It is essential for financial institutions to adopt these advanced methodologies to reduce the risk associated with lending decisions.
Research indicates that the quality and availability of data play a crucial role in the effectiveness of machine learning models in the context of loan approval. Taye (2023) focuses on the remarkable ability of deep learning to process large volumes of data efficiently. This is particularly important for creating reliable credit scoring systems. The investigation into various data sources, and the exploration of their potential implications, can enhance the ability of models to discern patterns indicative of credit behaviour. The focus on obtaining high-quality datasets, as identified by Bhatore et al. (2020), adds an additional layer of complexity and necessity. Institutions looking to develop strong machine learning approaches must ensure that they have access to comprehensive datasets that accurately reflect borrower behaviour and risk factors.
Another future direction for research in machine learning and loan approval involves the evaluation of various data modalities for credit risk assessment. Javeed et al. (2023) provide insights into the significance of using diverse types of data, such as transactional history, customer demographics, and even social media activity, to inform credit decisions. The exploration of multiple data modalities may facilitate a more holistic understanding of borrower profiles, ultimately leading to better decision-making processes. This multifaceted approach could counterbalance the limitations of relying solely on traditional credit scoring methods that often fail to capture a complete picture of borrower risk.

Research Gap
----------------------------------------
Despite considerable advances in machine learning for credit risk and loan approval prediction, several critical gaps persist in existing literature. Most prior studies treat risk scoring (regression) and loan approval classification (binary decision) as separate modelling tasks, resulting in fragmented pipelines that fail to produce consistent and unified outputs. This separation limits the interpretability and reliability of financial predictions in practice.

Another significant gap lies in the underutilisation of feature engineering specifically tailored to financial tabular data. While preprocessing steps such as encoding and scaling are commonly applied, the construction of domain-informed composite features—such as income stability ratios, debt stress indices, and credit experience scores—has been largely neglected. Such engineered indicators can substantially improve the signal quality for both regression and classification objectives.

Furthermore, existing research has inadequately explored attention-based tabular deep learning architectures for financial risk tasks. Conventional neural networks are often applied without mechanisms that offer feature-level interpretability, a critical requirement for regulatory compliance in lending institutions. Architectures that combine sequential attention with sparse feature selection can simultaneously improve predictive performance and transparency, yet their application to dual-task financial modelling remains sparse.

The integration of business rule governance layers into predictive systems is also largely absent from published work. Predictive models in isolation cannot meet the operational requirements of financial institutions, which demand policy-aligned override mechanisms alongside statistical predictions. Bridging the gap between model outputs and institutional lending policies within a single deployable framework is a direction that existing studies have not adequately addressed.

Finally, the generation of human-readable, compliance-ready decision explanations through large language models operating on top of risk scores is virtually unexplored. Combining structured numeric predictions with automated natural language underwriting reports represents a novel and practically valuable direction that current literature has yet to formalise or validate.
